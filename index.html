Dispatches from the Natural World
Tidings of Hope and Restoration from the Far Territories
Mexico's Jaguar Population Rises 30 Per Cent in Fourteen Years of Concerted Conservation
National Jaguar Conservation Programme · Mexico · 2026 Report
Related Tidings
The High Seas Treaty entered into force in January 2026, establishing legal protection for nearly two-thirds of the world's oceans — an area previously ungoverned by any international law.

Gratifying dispatches arrive from the Republic of Mexico, where a comprehensive fourteen-year programme of conservation hath produced results that must gladden the heart of every naturalist. Between 2010 and 2024, the jaguar population of that vast nation increased by thirty per cent, from some 4,100 animals to 5,326 individuals — a recovery achieved through the deployment of 920 motion-sensor cameras across fifteen states and the tireless efforts of rangers, scientists, and communities alike.

Key to the jaguar's resurgence hath been the protection of habitat corridors, the reduction of conflict between the great cats and local livestock farmers, and a remarkable rise in public awareness and pride in this most magnificent of the Americas' predators. The jaguar, long persecuted and retreating before the advance of civilisation, now prowls in greater numbers than at any time in living memory. A testament to what patient, persistent, and well-funded conservation can achieve.
